Key Terms

The following vocabulary is crucial to your understanding of health insurance. Additionally, a great resource for easy to understand terms is the Federal health care site, https://www.healthcare.gov/glossary/

Term Definition
Premiums The amount of money deducted from your paycheck to ensure that you have insurance. It is what you pay in order to be covered. The premium is the cost of being covered- not the cost of care.
Deductible For insurance plans, you need to spend a certain amount of money before the plan begins sharing the costs. This amount of money is called the deductible. Depending on the plan and it's setup, the deductible can be large or small. If the deductible is over $1,300 for an individual and $2,600 for a family, it's considered a "high deductible" plan, and eligible to be paired with a health savings account. Typically, plans with lower premiums have higher deductibles, and plans with higher premiums have lower deductibles.
Co-insurance Once you meet your deductible, your plan begins to share costs with you for doctor visits, procedures, etc. The plan pays a certain percentage of the cost, and you pay a certain percentage. The amount that you are paying is the co-insurance. For example, if your plan pays 90% toward a specialist visit, the 10% they don’t pay is your co-insurance. You pay co-insurance until you meet your out-of-pocket maximum (which can vary depending on whether you stay in-network or go out-of-network).
Out of Pocket Maximum The out of pocket maximum is the most you will pay in a single plan year. It does not include your premiums, or any service your plan does not cover. Once you reach this number, the plan pays 100% of all services. The maximum is typically different for individual coverage versus family coverage. The ACA puts limits on how high these can be.
Co-pay(ment) This is a fixed amount that you pay for a specific service. For example, despite what the full cost of a office visit to your doctor may be, you will always pay just $10 (or whatever the plan's co-pay is) instead. This can apply to prescriptions as well. Plans B and C do not have office visit co-pays, but once you meet your deductible, presscriptions go to co-pay tiers until you meet your out of pocket maximum.
In-Network This refers to a provider or a facility that is within a insurance plan’s “network of approved providers”. This means they have agreed to a negotiated discount rate for plan participants (that’s you) and are contractually obligated to comply and accept plan payments in full. You will pay less (and more reasonable amounts) when using an in-network doctor or facility.
Out-of-Network This refers to a provider or facility that is outside an insurance plan’s “network of approved providers”. They are not contracted to any rate or discount and they can charge however much they please, and you will not only typically pay more in general, but you will likely be stuck paying much more.
Dependent Basically, its your spouse and your children. The IRS gets a little more technical, but for most people, this is it. Click here to learn about our plan eligiblity.
Preventive Care These are routine health care things that you need to do to stay healthy and prevent future health issues. This includes annual physicals, well-woman exams, screenings. Under the ACA, preventive care costs are covered at 100%. Haven’t had a physical in years? Time to go.
